# Introduction

Alzheimer's disease (AD) is the most common neurodegenerative disorder, affecting over 50 million people worldwide. Genome-wide association studies have identified more than 75 risk loci, with pathway analyses consistently implicating innate immunity, endocytosis, and lipid metabolism (Bellenguez et al., 2022; Wightman et al., 2021). Integration of these genetic findings with cell-type-specific epigenomic data has established that AD risk variants are preferentially active in myeloid cells, including brain-resident microglia and peripheral monocytes/macrophages (Tansey et al., 2018; Novikova et al., 2021; Novikova et al., 2023). These observations have shifted the conceptual framework of AD from a purely neuronal disease to one with a central immune component.

Despite this progress, the spatial and organ-level architecture of AD genetic risk remains uncharacterized. Prior cell-type enrichment analyses relied on dissociated single-cell or bulk transcriptomic data, which lose spatial context and cannot assess how genetic risk distributes across anatomical structures within and between organs. Whether AD risk variants act exclusively through immune cells, or whether non-immune cell populations in peripheral organs also contribute, is unknown. The recent emergence of whole-body spatial transcriptomics (Chen et al., 2026) and spatial GWAS projection methods (Song et al., 2025) now enables systematic mapping of disease genetic risk at spatial resolution across the entire body — an approach that has not been applied to any neurodegenerative disease. Furthermore, whether the spatial pattern of AD genetic risk changes with aging, or whether it differs from that of other neurodegenerative diseases such as Parkinson's disease (PD) and amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS), has not been investigated.

Here, we applied gsMap to project AD GWAS signals onto whole-body Array-seq spatial transcriptomics data across 15 mouse organs, integrated with multi-age single-cell RNA-seq data spanning 1 to 30 months. We report that AD genetic risk is carried by tissue-resident macrophages across all organs, but that colonic epithelial cells harbor an independent AD risk program centered on APP processing genes — a finding absent in PD. These results identify two distinct peripheral pathways of AD genetic risk and provide spatial genetic evidence for host-mediated gut-brain axis involvement in AD.
