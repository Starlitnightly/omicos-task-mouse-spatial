# Discussion

This study provides the first whole-body, spatially resolved map of AD genetic risk across multiple organs and age points. Three principal findings emerge.

First, AD genetic risk is carried by tissue-resident macrophages in every organ examined. Kupffer cells in liver, alveolar macrophages in lung, and microglia in brain all showed strong heritability enrichment, driven by a shared set of myeloid transcription factor and phagocytic genes (TREM2, CD33, SPI1, PLCG2). This extends prior work showing AD risk variant enrichment in myeloid enhancers (Tansey et al., 2018; Novikova et al., 2021) by demonstrating that this enrichment manifests uniformly across tissue-resident macrophage populations in their native spatial contexts, not only in microglia. The finding that brain microglia ranked below peripheral macrophages in enrichment strength (before microglia enrichment correction) underscores that AD genetic risk is a systemic myeloid phenotype that converges on the brain, rather than originating there.

Second, colonic epithelial cells — specifically secretory cells and enterocytes — harbor an independent AD risk program that is not myeloid-driven. Gene-level analysis revealed that this enrichment was driven by APP, SORL1, PSEN1, ADAM10, CD2AP, and PICALM: genes that together constitute the core APP processing and endosomal trafficking machinery. This finding is consistent with prior reports that intestinal epithelial cells express APP and secrete Aβ peptides (Galloway et al., 2015), that Aβ disrupts intestinal tight junctions (Puig et al., 2015), and that enteric Aβ deposits accompany gut dysfunction in AD mouse models (Yeh et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2025). Our data extend these observations by providing the first genetic evidence that AD risk variants directly regulate amyloid-related genes in gut epithelial cells at spatial resolution. This suggests that peripheral Aβ production in the gut may be a genetically programmed feature of AD, not merely a consequence of systemic amyloid spread from the brain.

Third, multi-trait comparison revealed that the gut epithelial enrichment is preferentially associated with AD. PD showed no significant gut epithelial signal and was instead driven by oligodendrocytes and astrocytes in brain — consistent with PD genetics implicating lysosomal and mitochondrial pathways in glial cells (Nalls et al., 2019). ALS showed intermediate myeloid enrichment and a partial gut epithelial signal, suggesting some degree of shared peripheral mechanisms with AD. The myeloid enrichment gradient (AD ≫ ALS > PD) may reflect the relative contribution of innate immunity to each disease's genetic architecture.

Several limitations should be noted. The spatial transcriptomics data was from 6-week-old mice, and age-resolved analyses relied on CMAP computational mapping rather than direct multi-age spatial profiling. Cross-species inference from mouse to human, while supported by conserved myeloid biology, may not capture human-specific regulatory elements. The CMAP approach assigns single cells to spatial positions based on transcriptomic similarity, which may introduce mapping errors for rare cell types. Some organ-age combinations had limited cell numbers (e.g., Liver at 21 months: 367 cells), potentially reducing statistical power. Finally, gsMap assesses heritability enrichment at the gene level through S-LDSC, not individual causal variant identification, and functional validation of the gut epithelial APP processing pathway in human tissue is needed.

These findings have implications for AD research and therapeutics. The identification of colonic epithelium as a site of genetically regulated APP processing suggests that gut-targeted interventions — whether aimed at reducing intestinal Aβ production, strengthening epithelial barrier function, or modulating the interaction between epithelial-derived Aβ and mucosal immune cells — may represent a complementary therapeutic strategy. More broadly, the demonstration that AD has a dual peripheral genetic architecture (myeloid + gut epithelial), distinct from PD and ALS, argues for disease-specific rather than generic approaches to neurodegeneration.
